# The Discomfort Gap

> *Clarity is kindness. Nothing great happens until people are uncomfortable. And especially the best get better.*

Isaiah, this is the section of your report where the data speaks loudest — not because you're weak here, but because the pattern is so coherent that the coaching path practically draws itself. Before any of the numbers: you care about your people, genuinely and demonstrably. The evidence on that is unambiguous. The opportunity is to let that care do more work for them than it currently does. Care without expectation is just fondness. Care *with* expectation — the kind that says "I can see further into what you're capable of than you can right now" — is leadership.

The gap between where you are and where you're already set up to be shows up in three places. They look different on the surface. They share a single root, which is what makes them coachable together: they're all moments where you could invite someone into productive discomfort, and you instead absorb the discomfort yourself. Small changes in each one compound into large changes in how your whole team operates.

## Move 1 — Push discomfort instead of absorbing it

Start with the strength, because it's the foundation everything else gets built on. One of the categories where you scored highest, peer-relative, is **Cares About Others Not Their Approval** — and specifically Q131, where you said your leadership is admired because you focus on impact rather than perception. That is not a people-pleaser's answer. You are not leading by applause meter, and your data says so clearly. You are a leader whose people could trust that you're not going to fold to social pressure.

Which is exactly the starting material we need. The work in Move 1 is to put that trust into service of *higher* expectations for them, not lower ones.

The data tells us you're not there yet. The area called **Discomfort For Team** is one of the clearest gaps in your profile — four different questions all pointing in the same direction, including Q63 (where the ideal answer is that peak performance happens when teams are *uncomfortable*, and you leaned toward "comfortable"). **Discomfort For Self** is the mirror: you don't habitually step into discomfort yourself, either (Q79 and Q69, both scored toward the middle). And the algorithm lit a flag called **"Lower Standards for Others than Self"** — one of the most diagnostically specific flags in the whole system. It only fires when the pattern is broad enough to corroborate across many questions.

The two largest peer-relative gaps in your entire dataset live here. Q75 — *"My team knows the #1 overall corporate priority, the quantification of it, and the date we'll hit it"* — you answered toward "no." Q84 — *"I restrict my ideation; instead I mention the #1 priority in what feels like every conversation"* — same direction. Read them together and you can see the move: you have exceptional decision quality, you set great priorities, and then you don't repeat them often enough for the organization to internalize them. Your high-C, intellectually restless temperament probably makes repetition feel like laziness. It isn't. It's the discomfort tax of leadership — the self-discipline to keep saying the boring thing until your team is tired of hearing it, and then three more times after that. The Fred Smith line at the heart of Q84 captures it: *the main thing is to keep the main thing the main thing.*

Here's the reframe. The commentary on Q63 says it best: *"Great leaders have the same or higher expectations for others than they have for themselves, because they admire their followers."* You admire your people. That's not in question. The invitation is to admire them *louder* — to show them that you see more in them than they see in themselves, and that seeing more means asking more. **Clarity is kindness.** When you hold back the harder conversation, the harder standard, the unambiguous priority, you are not being gentle with them. You are declining to invest in their growth. A team whose leader admires them will lean into higher expectations. A team whose leader doesn't will lean away. Your data says your people would lean in.

**Small changes, big compounding.** The first move is tiny: pick one priority and state it at the start of every meeting for the next 30 days. That's it. No new framework, no transformation. Just repetition. Your Commitment to Routine score says you can absolutely do this — it's one of your strongest areas. Apply the same routine muscle you already have on yourself to something that benefits them.

## Move 2 — Let them own the urgency

This move starts with another strength worth celebrating first. Q81 — *"I am not the one creating the urgency in any departments"* — is your #5 highest strength, peer-relative. You understand, better than most leaders we see, that urgency pushed from the top creates dependency rather than ownership. You've already resisted the CEO pattern of manufacturing artificial fire drills. That's not nothing. That's the harder half of the principle already operating.

But here's where the data gets interesting, and where the coaching lives. Believing the right principle and *having it be true in your organization* are two different things. Q140 asks about the lowest level of commitment among your direct reports — you answered Acceptance (they recognize the priority is important even if they don't fully agree). The ideal is Identification (they feel the priority is connected to who they are). Q71 — Ask vs. Tell — you indicated your people lean toward asking. The ideal is telling, in the specific sense of *"here's what I'm going to do"* ownership. And Q51, about extreme fire in the belly, you answered toward low.

Taken together: you've removed the top-down urgency pattern (good), and the organic, bottom-up, Identification-level urgency hasn't grown up in its place (not yet). There's a middle state the commentary on Q81 names directly: *"urgency, for the most part, cannot be taught. Use DISC motivators and urgency standards to choose urgent lieutenants with their own desire to make a difference."* The lever is selection and reinforcement — not of your own urgency, but of theirs.

This connects to Move 1 in a precise way. Every time a lieutenant brings you an ambiguous situation and asks *"what should I do?"*, you have a choice. You can answer — and they walk away relieved, with the discomfort successfully transferred back to you. Or you can reflect it back: *"what do you think you're going to do? Tell me, then I'll react."* The second move is slightly uncomfortable for both of you. It's also the move that turns Acceptance into Identification over time.

Your Receptive motivator (your #1, well above the mainstream) and Intellectual motivator (your #2) make you a natural, generous explorer of other people's problems. That is a strength. It also makes you the most likely leader in any room to accept the "ask" frame when an "own" frame would serve the person better. You can keep the curiosity. Just redirect it — instead of exploring the problem, explore *their plan*.

**Small changes, big compounding.** For 30 days, when a direct report asks you what to do, respond with: *"Walk me through what you're planning."* That's the whole change. You're still available, you're still thoughtful, you're still the facilitator you already are — you've just shifted the locus of ownership by one inch. Watch what happens over the quarter.

## Move 3 — Step back from the operations

The third move is the biggest one on the surface and also the most forgiving, because it's the one where your Facilitative Mindset — which we'll come back to in a moment — has the most direct leverage.

The data here is direct. Q2 — *"When I disappear…"* — you answered at the low end; the ideal is "able to disappear." This is the sixth-highest impact item in your whole profile. Q9 — *"I let my direct reports make decisions I disagree with"* — you answered toward "no." Q15 — *"In team meetings, instead of inspiring with ideas and driving to solutions, I conduct"* — you answered toward "no." Q31 — *"I resist being involved in operations until I have to"* — same pattern. Q33, Q55, and two lit flags ("Holds Average Down Chain," "Hands-On Severity") all point at the same thing: you're still playing lead guitarist more often than conductor.

The strength hiding inside this pattern is worth naming. You have very high Extreme Ownership scores. You will not let things fall. Your team knows this about you. They also, probably without articulating it, have calibrated to it — they know that if something is ambiguous or hard, you'll pick it up. That calibration is why they're not growing at the rate they could.

The coaching principle here is the one that sits at the top of this section: *especially the best get better.* Your top performers do not need to be protected from the hard stuff. They need to be invited into it. The commentary on Q33 is worth reading slowly — it rejects the replace-the-bottom-10% frame entirely and replaces it with a commitment-to-growth frame where *"A players and below have to get better to have a seat on this team."* That is the standard you want them operating under. They won't get there while you're still holding the weight for them.

Q55's ideal is blunt but true: *"your job as a leader is to replace yourself."* That framing can land as a threat to identity for some leaders. For you, given your Receptive driver (the coaching motivator), it should land as permission. You are already motivated by learning and growth. This is the version of growth that serves the organization: building the system that doesn't need you.

**Small changes, big compounding.** Identify one decision this month that you'd normally make and hand it fully to a direct report. Not "here's my view, you decide" — just "you decide, tell me what you chose." Then actually let them choose, even if you'd have chosen differently. Q9's ideal behavior isn't "let them make every call" — it's "let them make calls you disagree with sometimes." Once a month is a revolution.

## Why this is already within reach for you

Here's the reason to close this section with confidence rather than warning. Hidden inside your profile is a category we haven't named yet, and it matters: **Risking by Facilitative Mindset**. Your score there is one of your highest strengths. You already lead by dialogue rather than direction. You already sublimate ego well. You already manage power and status with skill. Those are exactly the capabilities a leader needs to deliver discomfort *without* becoming the kind of leader who extracts it through dominance.

You don't need to become more alpha. You couldn't, and you shouldn't want to. You need to apply the facilitative skill you already have to the three moves above. That's not a transformation. It's a redirection.

The coaching reframe that unlocks all three moves at once: **your people will rise to the expectations you hold for them, because they can tell you actually hold them.** Your profile says you care deeply, and that comes through. Now let them feel the second half — that because you care, you expect more. Clarity is kindness. Discomfort is where growth lives. And especially the best get better.

You are, by every measure we can see, set up to do this well. The data just says you haven't yet. The work is small. The compounding is large.
