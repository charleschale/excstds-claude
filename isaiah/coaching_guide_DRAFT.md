# Isaiah Harvey — Integrated Coaching Guide

*A synthesis of TTI Talent Insights (behaviors + driving forces) and the Excellence Standards coaching model. Built around the ten behaviors where Isaiah is already effective enough to teach and the ten where deliberate practice will produce the largest lift.*

---

## How to read this guide

The Excellence Standards model identifies, for every respondent, the specific behaviors where they are already at a teaching level of mastery (the **Areas for Teaching**) and the specific behaviors where their effectiveness and their ranked impact on organizational outcomes are furthest apart (the **Areas for Impact**). These are not invented — each is a scored question with an established behavioral standard and a research-backed commentary.

The TTI profile tells us *why* each behavior sits where it does: whether it aligns with Isaiah's natural wiring (his DISC and Behavioral Hierarchy) or runs against it, and whether his primary Driving Forces support it or leave it dependent on deliberate effort.

The integration point is simple. For the teach list, we name which elements of his profile are making each strength sustainable. For the impact list, we name what's working against expression — and match each routine to the Driving Force that will carry it, because routines that serve Receptive (68), Intellectual (67), or Intentional (64) will stick, and routines framed as pure structure will not (Structured is his #12, Indifferent at 14).

## Part 1 — What you teach

Ten standards where Isaiah's Excellence Standards scoring ranks him highly enough that these are the behaviors he should be actively coaching others on. Each entry names the standard, the reason it works, the behaviors to pass on, and how his profile produces it.

### 1. Hold decisions open until you must make them *(Q123)*

**The standard:** *"I don't make an irreversible decision until I have to. I prize the optionality inherent in studying a problem and not acting until it is necessary."*

**Why it works:** Great decision-makers recognize that every decision has an ideal window, and that deliberately deferring captures more information, more stakeholder alignment, and more market signal. Then they act vigorously when timing arrives. The pattern is: keep options open, but never lose the moment.

**What to teach:** The discipline of patience followed by vigor. Names for the moments when the window begins to close. The difference between deferral and indecisiveness.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your C=81, Analysis=95, Persistence=90 make optionality-preservation effortless. Your Intellectual (67, P2) rewards the gathering of data and the careful reasoning about timing. Your Receptive (68, P1) keeps you open to reframing. This is the single cleanest alignment in your entire profile between wiring and standard.

### 2. Enable your leaders to reach their full potential *(Q137)*

**The standard:** *"My goal is to enable my leaders to reach their full potential."*

**Why it works:** Leaders who orient around their people's ceiling rather than their own performance create sustainable development cultures. The orientation matters as much as the tactics.

**What to teach:** Framing leadership as elevation of others. Making that orientation visible in language and choices.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your Intentional (64, P3) — "driven to assist others for a specific purpose" — is the defining fit here. This is not generic helpfulness; it's the specific investment in specific people your profile genuinely motivates. Your belief is stronger than most; the expression is the work you'll see later in Part 2.

### 3. Focus on impact over perception *(Q131)*

**The standard:** Your leadership is admired because you focus on impact rather than perception — you make necessary decisions without hesitation and act from conviction rather than consensus.

**Why it works:** Leaders who lead by impact set a tone where the team orients around outcomes rather than optics. It's a rare stance and an immediately legible one.

**What to teach:** *"Makes necessary decisions without hesitation. Takes unpopular stands on principle. Acts from conviction rather than consensus. Puts improvement before approval."* — lift the target behaviors directly.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your Interaction=25 and low-I (25) mean you're not wired to chase approval. Your Intentional (64) gives the caring part without the need-to-be-liked part. You are, in profile terms, temperamentally suited to this stance. Most leaders have to fight for it; you have it by wiring.

### 4. Don't take stances outside the core mission *(Q119)*

**The standard:** *"When leaders take a stance and attempt to do the 'right' thing about issues unconnected to the core mission, everyone loses — especially the leader."*

**Why it works:** Mission discipline protects both the leader's credibility and the team's focus. Taking positions on peripheral issues dilutes attention and invites division.

**What to teach:** The distinction between mission-relevant and mission-adjacent issues. How to deflect requests to weigh in on peripheral matters without seeming uncaring.

**Why it's natural for you:** Analysis=95 and Following Policy=80 make mission discipline a native behavior. Your Structured driver is Indifferent (14) — you don't impose rigid rules on others — but Analysis gives you the internal filter that distinguishes mission from noise.

### 5. Don't be the one creating the urgency *(Q81)*

**The standard:** *"I am not the one creating the urgency in any departments. Areas of the business aren't dependent on me for pace."*

**Why it works:** Top-down urgency creates dependency and reactivity. Intrinsic urgency at every level creates responsiveness and ownership.

**What to teach:** *"Enshrine that teams own their pace and energy. Hire for intrinsic urgency."* How to recognize whether urgency is being generated or imported.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your Urgency=38 (1 SD low) and Versatility=30 mean you do not naturally drive tempo. That wiring, normally a liability, here becomes a strength — you are *incapable* of being the CEO who creates artificial urgency because it is not in your register to do so. Your Receptive (68) values intrinsic motivation. The belief is intact and visible.

### 6. Grow by taking informed risks *(Q68)*

**The standard:** You prefer a low-probability path with high growth potential over a high-probability path with limited upside — within the frame of informed decision-making.

**Why it works:** Organizations grow on the back of leaders who accept asymmetric bets when the upside is meaningful. Risk-aversion at the top propagates downward and caps growth.

**What to teach:** The math of expected value under asymmetric payoffs. How to distinguish informed risk from reckless risk.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your Intellectual (67, P2) is rewarded by the reasoning involved. Your C=81 ensures the informed part. The combination produces a particular kind of measured boldness that teaches well.

### 7. Give feedforward more than feedback *(Q25)*

**The standard:** *"I give 'feedforward' more than feedback. When giving feedback, I focus primarily on future actions rather than past mistakes."*

**Why it works:** Past-focused feedback activates defensiveness; future-focused framing activates agency. Same content, different behavioral response.

**What to teach:** How to reframe a piece of critical feedback as a forward-action. The vocabulary swap.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your Receptive (68) is about new methods and approaches — feedforward is literally that applied to coaching conversations. Your Intentional (64) gives you the specific-to-this-person targeting that makes feedforward land.

### 8. Keep daily routines that compound *(Q24)*

**The standard:** *"There are routines that I have done every day for years."*

**Why it works:** Compounding is the leader's most under-rated tool. Daily routines create both personal reliability and organizational pattern.

**What to teach:** How to select routines that serve your primary drivers so they're sustainable. The difference between routines imposed by discipline and routines pulled by purpose.

**Why it's natural for you:** Persistence=90 and Consistent=85 and Organized Workplace=95 — this is the top of your Behavioral Hierarchy expressing itself exactly as it should. Worth noting you do this *despite* Structured being your Indifferent driver — the routines you keep serve one of your Primary drivers (learning, specific impact, exploring methodology), not structure for its own sake. Teaching this well means helping others pick routines the same way.

### 9. Remove misaligned team members quickly *(Q120)*

**The standard:** *"When a team member can't surrender their distaste for the leader and organization, they must be removed quickly. Waiting and hoping for the best is how good organizations decay."*

**Why it works:** Misaligned team members don't stay neutral; they pull others. Quick, decisive action protects the rest of the team.

**What to teach:** How to distinguish disagreement (healthy) from disloyalty (terminal). The practice of moving quickly without becoming paranoid.

**Why it's natural for you:** This one is carried more by your belief system than by your wiring — your C=81 and Analysis=95 help you see the pattern clearly, but the decisive action required does not come from your Low-I and mean-D. Worth watching this as a behavior you express as a held principle, which can slip under pressure.

### 10. Define success by meaningful accomplishment *(Q115)*

**The standard:** A person is successful if they have grown, contributed, and measurably affected others — not by extrinsic markers.

**Why it works:** The definition you use propagates. Leaders who define success by extrinsic markers get teams that optimize for optics; leaders who define it by contribution get teams that optimize for impact.

**What to teach:** The operational definition of success you hold. How you evaluate people against it.

**Why it's natural for you:** Intentional (64) aligned with specific contribution. Intellectual (67) aligned with growth. Your definition of success is emitted from your drivers.

## Part 2 — What to work on

Ten standards where the gap between your current effectiveness and the research-established standard is largest, ranked by impact on organizational outcomes. For each one, the behaviors to install are already specified in the Excellence Standards model — your work is to build them as routines, and the frame below indicates which of your Driving Forces will make each routine stick.

### 1. Challenge your direct reports daily *(Q134 — Impact #1)*

**The standard:** *"I challenge my direct reports every day or almost every day."*

**Why it matters:** The least effective leaders avoid difficult conversations and express dissatisfaction indirectly, which erodes accountability across the company. Daily challenge makes difficult conversations normal rather than exceptional, forces you to address issues when they're small, and prevents the accumulation of unaddressed problems.

**What to do:** *"Address issues same day even if setting a formal conversation for another day. Have growth conversations daily. Raise concerns immediately. Give specific, timely feedback. Make tough talks routine."*

**Your profile:** Low-I (25), Interaction=25, Competitive=50 — none of your wiring pulls you toward daily challenge. This is a top-quadrant not-natural + ineffective behavior. Your answer of 4 on a 1-is-ideal scale is not a surprise given the wiring. **Fuel: Intentional (64).** Frame this as *specific investment in specific people*, not as broad toughness. You are not becoming a confrontational CEO; you are acting on the care you already feel.

### 2. Drive commitment to Identification-level *(Q140 — Impact #2)*

**The standard:** The lowest level of commitment to priorities among your direct reports should be Identification — they feel the priority is connected to who they are. Not Compliance, not Acceptance, not Agreement.

**Why it matters:** Teams tops out at the commitment level their leader tolerates. Acceptance-level commitment executes under direction but does not self-drive when the leader is absent.

**What to do:** Notice explicitly which commitment level each direct report is operating at. Name it in your development conversations. Build a practice of moving people up the ladder, one level at a time.

**Your profile:** Your Proactive Belief In People area is your single largest category gap. Your low Competitive and low Urgency make this the hardest quadrant. **Fuel: Intentional (64) + Intellectual (67).** Frame this as diagnostic work — you are reading each person's commitment level carefully, which is an analytical task your Intellectual driver enjoys.

### 3. Peak performance lives in discomfort *(Q63 — Impact #3)*

**The standard:** *"Peak performance happens when teams are uncomfortable, not comfortable."*

**Why it matters:** Nothing great happens until people and teams are pushed out of their comfort zones. Leaders who keep people comfortable cap their development at whatever level arrived naturally.

**What to do:** *"Create productive tension daily. Push teams beyond their comfort. Increase pressure with capability. Design stretch into every role. Make discomfort purposeful."*

**Your profile:** You answered 2 where the ideal is 5. Interaction=25 and People-Oriented=45 mean your default read of "comfortable team" is positive rather than a warning. **Fuel: Intentional (64).** The reframe: you are not making people uncomfortable for the sake of pressure; you are doing the uncomfortable thing of raising expectations because you admire them enough to believe they can meet them. Clarity is kindness. Especially the best get better.

### 4. Your team knows the #1 priority exactly *(Q75 — Impact #5)*

**The standard:** *"My team knows the #1 overall corporate priority, the quantification of it, and the date we will hit it."*

**Why it matters:** Without a clear north star, people work on what they think is important rather than what actually is. Trade-offs become hard, motivation suffers. The top-of-profile gap here — your largest peer-relative gap — indicates this is the single highest-leverage move in your whole portfolio.

**What to do:** *"State the #1 priority in every meeting. Quantify the primary goal specifically. Set clear achievement dates. Connect all decisions to top priority. Resist introducing competing ideas. Reference the priority naturally. Make the choice visible daily."*

**Your profile:** Your Analysis=95 and Organized=95 mean you have the priority clear in your own head. The gap is in repetition and emphasis — behaviors that require Urgency (you score 38) and Interaction (25), neither of which pulls you. **Fuel: Intellectual (67).** Frame priority-repetition as a mechanism for transferring your analytical clarity into the team's shared operating system.

### 5. Build the organization that runs without you *(Q2 — Impact #6)*

**The standard:** *"When I disappear, the business runs at full performance."*

**Why it matters:** An organization that cannot function without you is exposed to risk, but more importantly, it reflects incomplete development of the team around you and systems below you. The standard asks you to be able to step away for weeks without quality degrading.

**What to do:** The target behavior here is framed broadly — *"replacing oneself"* — because this is a system-level outcome rather than a single routine. Practically: identify the decisions and operations that depend on you, and systematically reduce that dependency.

**Your profile:** C=81, Analysis=95, Persistence=90 all conspire to keep you engaged in quality assurance. Versatility=30 and Frequent Change=32 mean you don't naturally let go once you've committed attention. **Fuel: Intellectual (67) + Receptive (68).** Reframe disappearance as a diagnostic experiment — each time you step back, you learn something about your system's robustness. That is your two strongest drivers at work simultaneously.

### 6. Resist involvement until it is truly needed *(Q31 — Impact #7)*

**The standard:** *"I resist being involved in the actual operation or process of getting things done until I have to — and when I do, I go all-in."*

**Why it matters:** Hands-off and eyes-on until the moment you're needed, then full engagement. The discipline of restraint is as important as the vigor of engagement.

**What to do:** *"Trust your teams to execute. Let others own their work. Stay out until absolutely needed. Go all-in when truly required."*

**Your profile:** Your wiring wants to help. C=81 wants quality, Analysis=95 sees problems early, Persistence=90 stays with them. **Fuel: Receptive (68).** Frame the restraint as respect for others' methodology — you are not abandoning them; you are holding space for them to find their own path. That aligns with Receptive directly.

### 7. Set standards by what you tolerate *(Q33 — Impact #8)*

**The standard:** The question of replacing the bottom 10% is the wrong frame. The right frame is commitment to growth at every level — *"especially the best get better"* — and action on misalignment when it appears.

**Why it matters:** What you tolerate becomes your ceiling. Leaders who accept comfortable performance at any level are capping the whole team.

**What to do:** *"Actively develop or move out underperformers. Create clear performance expectations up front. Address performance gaps as soon as they appear. Take action rather than hoping for improvement. Make personnel changes before they become necessary."*

**Your profile:** Your Competitive=50 and Urgency=38 don't drive the decisive action required. **Fuel: Intentional (64).** Frame this as honoring the rest of the team — every tolerated gap sends a message to the high performers about what the standard actually is.

### 8. Restrict ideation; keep repeating the priority *(Q84 — Impact #9)*

**The standard:** *"I restrict my ideation. Instead, I mention the #1 priority in what feels like every conversation."*

**Why it matters:** Your analytical range is wide. Your team's bandwidth for receiving new ideas is narrower than you'd guess. The discipline of withholding the new thing in favor of repeating the main thing is an act of leadership restraint.

**What to do:** Same target behaviors as Q75 — the #1 priority everywhere, competing ideas held back.

**Your profile:** Intellectual=67 is your #2 driver, and it pulls you toward new ideas constantly. This routine asks you to *withhold* what your driver pulls toward. **Fuel: Intentional (64).** Frame this as the cost you pay to give your team clarity — a specific investment in their ability to execute.

### 9. Tell, don't ask *(Q71 — Impact #10)*

**The standard:** The best team members operate from a *"tell"* mindset — *"here's what I'm going to do"* — not an *"ask"* mindset. The leader's job is to receive that telling, challenge it, and welcome input without requiring permission-seeking.

**Why it matters:** Asking puts ownership on the leader; telling puts ownership where it belongs. Your team's default right now is ask.

**What to do:** *"Present plans, don't ask for them. Take initiative without prompting. Drive direction proactively. Keep leaders informed, not consulted. Own outcomes completely. Welcome input without seeking permission. Make decisions, then discuss them."*

**Your profile:** Your Receptive (68) *wants* to be asked — it enjoys the exchange of methodologies. That tilts your team toward asking you. **Fuel: Receptive (68) redirected.** Keep the curiosity; shift its object. Instead of curious about the question, become curious about *their plan* before they share the question. The curiosity is the same; the ownership location moves.

### 10. Set standards by not tolerating the gap *(Q59 — Impact #13)*

**The standard:** You don't raise standards by declaring them; you raise them by declining to tolerate the gap when it appears.

**Why it matters:** Every gap not addressed is an implicit ratification of the lower standard. The act of addressing is the standard.

**What to do:** Paired target behaviors with Q33 and Q134 — address, develop, move out, expect growth.

**Your profile:** Same not-natural pattern as the other Pushing Accountability items. **Fuel: Intentional (64).** Frame as respect for the people operating above the gap — they deserve a standard that's actually held.

## How the two lists connect

Notice the mirror. Four of your top ten teach items are about holding open, holding restraint, holding belief — decisions not made until necessary, urgency not imposed, missions not drifted from, success not chased by markers. These are the *holding* strengths your wiring produces effortlessly.

Seven of your top ten impact items are about *pushing* — pushing priority repetition, pushing discomfort, pushing challenge, pushing ownership, pushing the team past your own presence. These are not in your wiring and will not arrive by willpower. They arrive by routines fueled by your actual Primary drivers: Receptive (new methodologies), Intellectual (knowledge generation), Intentional (specific impact on specific people).

The integrated coaching path is therefore not to become a different kind of leader. It is to use the holding strengths you already teach as the foundation on which the pushing routines get built — carried not by the discipline you don't motivationally have, but by the curiosity, learning, and specific care you actually do.
