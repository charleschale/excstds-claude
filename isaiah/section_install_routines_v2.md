# Where deliberate routines do the work

> *What comes naturally gets done on its own. What doesn't, doesn't — unless a routine carries it.*

Your DISC and Behavioral Hierarchy tell us, with remarkable precision, where the current of your day naturally carries you. You sit at 81 on C and 95 on both Analysis and Organized Workplace — top of the population on both. You sit at 25 on Interaction (bottom of the population), 30 on Versatility, 32 on Frequent Change, and 38 on Urgency. That profile is not a flaw; it's the shape of a CEO whose natural strengths are rigor, system-building, and disciplined decision-making. Those are exactly the strengths your Excellence Standards data confirms you express — Organizational Decision Making at +3.0, Personal Reliability at +1.49. The system works; the current carries you there without effort.

This section is about the behaviors the Excellence Standards data says are not being expressed, where the Behavioral Hierarchy also tells us the current does not naturally flow. You are not going to will these behaviors into being. You are not going to produce them by deciding to try harder. People don't produce behaviors that are five rungs down their own hierarchy by intending to. They produce them by building routines that carry the behavior the way the current otherwise would.

The good news is that for you specifically, we know exactly what fuel each routine needs. Your Primary Driving Forces — Receptive (68), Intellectual (67), and Intentional (64) — are what energize you. Your Structured driver is Indifferent (14, dead last). That means routines framed as "be more disciplined" or "impose more structure" will not take; routines framed as serving curiosity, learning, or specific people you care about will. Each of the four below is anchored to a Primary driver that will sustain it.

## Routine 1 — Priority repetition *(fuel: Intellectual)*

**The behavior:** State the organizational #1 priority, its measurable target, and its date at the top of every meeting you lead, and reference it when any significant decision comes up.

**Why it's not natural:** The behavior requires deliberate verbal repetition. Your top-of-population Analysis and Organized Workplace say you have already worked the priority out with rigor. Saying it again feels, to you, like noise after the signal is done. To your team, hearing it once is what noise sounds like — signal has not yet arrived. Q75 and Q84 both point here: your team is not where it needs to be on clarity of the #1 priority, and you don't yet use constant repetition as a leadership tool.

**Why it will stick if framed right:** Repetition as discipline won't motivate you — Structured is your Indifferent #12. But repetition as the *mechanism by which shared understanding is constructed* is exactly the kind of thing your Intellectual driver is built to pursue. You're not repeating to discipline; you're repeating to *transfer knowledge into the operating system of the team.* That's a problem worth your analytical attention.

**The routine:** At the start of every meeting for 30 days, you state the #1 priority, its metric, and its date in the first 90 seconds. When any decision point comes up, you name the priority as the frame: *"Given the #1 priority is X by Y date, the question is…"* After 30 days, you keep doing this in major meetings only; by then your team has internalized the cadence and it becomes theirs.

**The coaching idea:** Clarity is kindness. When you hold back repetition because you've already said it once, you're not being efficient — you're withholding the thing that lets them make good decisions on your behalf when you're not in the room.

## Routine 2 — The daily stretch invitation *(fuel: Intentional)*

**The behavior:** Once per working day, identify one specific person performing well and ask them to do something slightly beyond their current capability. Same-day delivery, specific ask.

**Why it's not natural:** Your Competitive score is 50 (mean), your People-Oriented is 45 (below mean), and your Interaction is 25 (bottom of the population). The DISC wiring for "push this person visibly into discomfort" is not present. Your natural current says: let them be comfortable, give them space, respect their autonomy. The Excellence Standards data, across four separate questions in the Discomfort For Team area and the lit flag "Lower Standards for Others than Self," says that respect is showing up as something less useful than respect — it's showing up as absence of challenge.

**Why it will stick if framed right:** Your Intentional driver (64, P3) is explicit: *"driven to assist others for a specific purpose."* Not generic helpfulness — specific assistance to specific people with specific outcomes in mind. That is exactly the mental model this routine requires. You are not becoming a pushy leader; you are being the coach who sees further into one specific person's potential than they see into it themselves, and who acts on that sight today.

**The routine:** Every working day, pick one person you admire — someone high on your list, not someone struggling. Tell them: *"Here's something I'd like you to take on."* Something slightly beyond their current proven capability. Deliver it warmly and specifically — your Intentional driver is generous, and it shows. One person per day. Track for 30 days.

**The coaching idea:** Especially the best get better. Your top performers are not fragile; they are the ones most energized by being seen accurately and asked for more. The commentary on Q79 is explicit: top performers feed off being pushed, provided the push lands as investment rather than extraction. Your demonstrated care is exactly what makes it land as investment. Use it.

## Routine 3 — The ownership reflection *(fuel: Receptive)*

**The behavior:** When a direct report asks you *"what should I do?"* or *"what do you think?"*, your first response is *"Walk me through what you're planning"* — before you offer any substantive answer.

**Why it's not natural:** Your Interaction score is 25 and your Versatility is 30 — both bottom-of-population. Your Urgency is 38. The natural impulse in an ambiguous moment is *not* to prolong the exchange by requiring their plan first — it's to close the loop with your own clear answer so the conversation can end and work can resume. Q71 and Q140 show this pattern: your team leans toward "ask" rather than "tell," and their commitment tops out at Acceptance rather than Identification. Your Proactive Belief In People area is your single largest category gap at −1.87.

**Why it will stick if framed right:** Reframe what's happening. You are not disciplining the team into ownership. You are *exploring their methodology* — which is precisely what your Receptive driver (68, P1, your single highest score across the whole instrument) is built to do. Receptive is defined as driven by new ideas and methods. Every "walk me through what you're planning" is an invitation for a new methodology to reveal itself. Your team will tell, you will be genuinely curious, and the ownership will transfer automatically in the asking.

**The routine:** For 30 days, your default opening response to any "what should I do?" is: *"Walk me through what you're planning."* Then listen with your actual curiosity — which is not in short supply. Challenge, ask, engage — but they propose first. If they say *"I don't have a plan"*, the follow-up is *"OK, walk me through how you'd build one."*

**The coaching idea:** The people who believe in themselves most are the ones whose leaders proved they already do. You can't give someone ownership by declaring it. You can give it to them by reliably refusing to take it from them.

## Routine 4 — Weekly disappearance *(fuel: Intellectual + Receptive)*

**The behavior:** Each week, identify one decision you would normally make and hand it fully to a direct report. *"You decide. Let me know what you chose."* Let them choose, even if you would have chosen differently.

**Why it's not natural:** This is the hardest of the four. Your C is 81, your Analysis 95, your Persistence 90 — your wiring tells you that quality of outcome depends on your engagement. The Excellence Standards data backs that up as a genuine risk: Q2 ("When I disappear…") is the sixth-largest gap in your data, Q9 ("I let my direct reports make decisions I disagree with") is a gap, two flags are lit ("Holds Average Down Chain" and "Hands-On Severity"). Your Versatility is 30 and Frequent Change is 32 — you do not naturally enjoy rapidly moving off of one thing once engaged. All of it conspires to keep you in the operation.

**Why it will stick if framed right:** Stop framing disappearance as abandonment of rigor. Start framing it as the single highest-information experiment you can run on your team. You hand them a decision and you get back a data point: *how did they think about it, how did they execute, how close to your answer was theirs.* That is a knowledge-generation activity — Intellectual's bread and butter. It is also a test of new methodologies — Receptive's bread and butter. You are not giving up control; you are running the diagnostic that tells you what your system is actually capable of without you in the loop.

**The routine:** Every Monday, pick one decision this week that you'd normally make. Assign it: *"You decide. Tell me what you chose."* Let them choose even when you disagree. The Q9 research is specific on this — *letting direct reports make decisions you disagree with* is not the same as delegation. It's a separate, harder behavior. Do it once a week. By the end of a quarter you'll have run 12 diagnostics. By the end of a year, 50.

**The coaching idea:** Your job is to replace yourself. That's not a threat; it's the metric. The organization that functions well without you is the organization that functions well with you. The weekly disappearance is the practice that makes both true.

## Why these four will compound

Every routine above uses a Primary Driving Force you actually have as the fuel. None of them asks you to be more dominant, more social, more extraverted, or more structured. You aren't any of those things, and trying to become them would fail. What you *are* is curious, analytical, and selectively committed to the specific people you care about. Those three drivers, directed precisely, produce all four routines without requiring a transformation.

What's compounding is not the difficulty — each individual routine is small. What compounds is the signal your team reads off of the change. A priority repeated daily becomes their priority. A daily stretch assignment to one person becomes a norm where the whole team expects to grow. An ownership reflection, run every time someone asks you a question, becomes a team that arrives with plans instead of questions. A weekly disappearance becomes a team that knows it can run without you, which is the only evidence that lets you actually run without them.

Small changes. Daily and weekly cadence. A quarter's patience. Your Persistence (90) and Consistent (85) say you will absolutely execute them if you decide to. The only decision is to start.
