# Where deliberate routines do the work

> *What's motivated gets done. What's not motivated needs a system.*

There's a useful distinction between the two instruments in this report. The TTI measures what pulls you — what you're naturally drawn toward and will find energy in. The Excellence Standards measure what you actually do — the behaviors that, across thousands of leaders, correlate with effectiveness. Put them side by side and four possibilities emerge: you're pulled toward it *and* doing it, you're pulled toward it but not doing it, you're doing it without being pulled toward it, or you're neither pulled nor doing it.

This section is about the last one. It's the most important section in the report for a practical reason: these are the areas where willpower, good intentions, and self-awareness won't get you there. Your motivators don't pull you, so the natural current of your day doesn't move you in this direction. The Excellence Standards say the behaviors aren't expressed, so whatever you've tried hasn't produced them consistently. The only thing that reliably works in this quadrant is the same thing that works in any domain where motivation is absent: **deliberate routines** that make the behavior the default instead of the exception.

This is not a weakness section. These are not character problems. They're structural mismatches between what your temperament pulls you toward and what effective leadership requires in specific moments. Structural mismatches get solved by structure.

## The pattern in one sentence

Your motivators — Receptive (#1, significantly above mainstream) and Intellectual (#2) — pull you toward dialogue, exploration, analysis, and generous inquiry. They do not pull you toward imposing discomfort, forcing urgency, or stepping back from a system that still relies on you. Those are Commanding-driver behaviors, and Commanding is not one of your top drivers. That's not a deficit. It's a fact about your wiring. The work is to build routines that produce those behaviors without requiring you to become someone you aren't.

Four specific routines would close the largest gaps in this quadrant. Each one is deliberately small — small enough that the routine itself, not the willpower, carries the behavior.

## Routine 1 — The priority repetition

**The behavior:** State the #1 organizational priority, its measurable target, and its date in every meeting you lead, and reference it as the frame for every significant decision.

**Why it's in this quadrant:** Your data says you don't do this consistently. Q75 shows your team isn't clear on the #1 priority in the way the data-backed ideal calls for. Q84 shows you don't yet use constant repetition of the main thing as a leadership tool. Neither of these is for lack of having priorities — your decision-making is one of your top strengths. The issue is the repetition itself. Receptive-dominant leaders tend to find repetition intellectually unsatisfying. You've already analyzed it; saying it again feels redundant. To the team, saying it once feels optional.

**The routine:** Before every meeting, write the priority at the top of your agenda. Say it in the first 90 seconds. Say it again when any decision point comes up. For 30 days. After 30 days your team will have internalized it enough that you can ease off; after 90 days it becomes their vocabulary, not just yours. Clarity is kindness — giving them the priority clearly, repeatedly, is one of the most generous things you can do as their leader.

## Routine 2 — The discomfort invitation

**The behavior:** Make productive discomfort a daily, visible part of how you lead, rather than something that only happens under pressure.

**Why it's in this quadrant:** Discomfort For Team is one of the areas where your Excellence Standards data is clearest about ineffective expression, and it's the area most unambiguously not in your motivator wiring. Your Intentional driver makes you want to help people; Commanding would make you want to stretch them; the first dominates in your profile and the second doesn't. So when a direct report is comfortable, your natural pull is to leave them comfortable. The Excellence Standards data — and every piece of evidence about how top performers actually grow — says that's the move that costs them.

Here's the coaching principle that matters for you specifically: *especially the best get better.* Your top performers are not fragile. They are the ones most energized by being challenged. Q79's research is explicit: top performers feed off being pushed, provided it's pushing (investing in their growth) rather than pressuring (using stress to extract performance). Your demonstrated care — the strength you already have in Cares About Others Not Their Approval — is exactly the foundation that makes pushing land as investment rather than extraction.

**The routine:** Once per day, identify one person who is performing well and ask them to do something slightly beyond their current capability. "Here's what I'd like you to take on." Not "would you like to." Not "when you're ready." Today. This week. Small stretches, daily, compound into a team culture where stretching is the norm. The routine is: one daily stretch assignment, delivered clearly and warmly, to someone you already admire.

## Routine 3 — The ownership reflection

**The behavior:** When a direct report asks you what to do, reflect the question back as *"what are you planning to do?"* before answering.

**Why it's in this quadrant:** Q71 and Q140 show your team defaults to Acceptance-level commitment and an "ask" orientation rather than ownership. The Proactive Belief In People area is your single largest gap — not because you don't believe in your people, but because the specific behavior of proactively pushing the ownership onto them isn't in your Receptive-Intellectual wiring. Receptive says "tell me more about what you're thinking" — which is great as *response*. Proactive Belief says "you figure it out and tell me" — which requires an active forward push, a Commanding-adjacent move.

The good news: you can achieve the outcome using your actual strengths. You don't need to become a dominant "figure it out yourself" leader. You need to turn your natural curiosity outward in a specific way.

**The routine:** For 30 days, when any direct report asks *"what should I do?"* or *"what do you think?"*, your first response is always: *"Walk me through what you're planning."* Then listen, engage, challenge — but they propose first. This turns your Receptive curiosity from a default that enables asking into a mechanism that enables telling. The behavior is the same ("I'm curious about your thinking"); the object changes (their plan, not just their question).

## Routine 4 — The weekly disappearance

**The behavior:** Deliberately remove yourself from one decision per week that you would ordinarily make.

**Why it's in this quadrant:** Q2 ("When I disappear...") is the sixth-largest gap in your data. Q9, Q15, and Q55 all cluster around the same pattern: you are still the lead guitarist more often than the conductor. Two lit flags — Holds Average Down Chain, Hands-On Severity — corroborate. The pull here is strong and structural: high-C temperament wants the quality to be right, and your Receptive driver makes you want to engage with interesting problems. Both of those pulls, working together, keep you in the operation.

The Excellence Standards research on this is striking. *"Your job as a leader is to replace yourself"* — that framing has been the foundational standard of this model since its first iteration. It doesn't mean you're not mission-critical. It means the quality of your leadership is measured by how well your organization runs *without* your constant presence. Right now, by the data, that measure is low. The routine that closes the gap is incremental, not a grand renunciation.

**The routine:** Every Monday, pick one decision this week that you'd normally make and assign it fully to a direct report. *"You decide. Let me know what you chose."* Then actually let them choose, even when you'd have chosen differently. Q9's research is precise on this: letting direct reports make decisions you disagree with is a specific, measurable behavior, not a vague posture. Once a week for a quarter. By month three your team has made a dozen decisions without you, which is both more capability-building than any coaching conversation and more evidence of their trustworthiness than you currently have.

## Why this is a high-confidence bet

There's a specific reason to believe these four routines will produce outsized results for you, and it's hiding inside your strengths rather than your gaps. Your **Facilitative Mindset** score is one of your highest. You lead by dialogue, you sublimate ego well, you manage power and status with skill. Those are exactly the capabilities a leader needs to deliver the four routines above *without* becoming someone whose temperament they can't support.

The alternative frame — that you need to become more Commanding, more dominant, more alpha — is wrong. You can't, and you shouldn't try. What you can do is use your existing facilitative strength as the delivery mechanism for behaviors that don't come naturally. The priority repetition gets delivered conversationally. The discomfort invitation gets delivered as investment in someone you already admire. The ownership reflection uses your curiosity as the lever. The weekly disappearance gets framed as trust extended, not abdication.

These are routines, not transformations. They will feel mechanical at first. That's the point. Motivated behaviors don't need routines because motivation carries them. Unmotivated behaviors need routines *precisely because* motivation doesn't carry them. The routine is the substitute for the pull that isn't there — and over time, the behavior becomes automatic enough that the routine fades into background, which is the version of mastery we're actually aiming at.

Small changes. Daily. For a quarter. Compounding.
