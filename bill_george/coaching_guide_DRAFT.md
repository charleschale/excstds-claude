## How to read this guide

The organizing principle is that **especially the best get better.** The Excellence Standards exist because great leaders keep practicing — they never graduate from the behaviors that created the excellence in the first place. This guide is not a test of capability or a report card. It is a practice tool, applied at a specific moment to surface where the next rep lives.

The standards themselves are universal. *"Believe in people in advance of themselves"* is the same standard whether the person is a direct report, a grandchild, or a mentee you've taken on at 83. *"Clarity is kindness"* is the same standard in a board meeting or a family dinner. The instrument is calibrated on workplace data, but the behaviors travel. For a leader at your stage — established, post-primary-career, with influence that extends well beyond any single role — the practice ground for the impact routines below is probably as much personal as it is professional. Where you apply these matters less than whether you apply them; the standards don't get softer in any sphere.

The scoring signal the guide uses is Z|algo — a standardized score derived from data on thousands of leaders that indicates how strongly each of your answers aligns with the patterns that predict value-creating leadership. Your overall Z|algo of +0.60 places you above the population average against that pattern. The ten impact items below are simply the ten places where the score says the next rep matters most.

## Part 1 — What you teach

Ten standards where you are already effective enough to coach others. Each entry names the standard, the reason it works, what to pass on, and how your profile produces it.

### 1. Spend your time with customers, not in the office *(Q135)*

**The standard:** Leaders spend a meaningful share of time with the people their organization serves.

**Why it works:** Customer contact is the oxygen of strategy. Leaders who maintain it make better decisions, spot category shifts earlier, and model the outward-facing orientation that keeps organizations from becoming self-absorbed.

**What to teach:** The discipline of forcing customer contact into the calendar when the internal demands would otherwise crowd it out. Your specific practices for how you structure that contact.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your Interaction (80) and People-Oriented (70) make customer contact energizing rather than depleting. Your Altruistic driver (57, P3) reframes customer time as service to the broader good rather than as transactional meetings. The combination is rare — most CEOs either love people or love strategy, not both.

### 2. Challenge your direct reports every day *(Q134)*

**The standard:** *"I challenge my direct reports every day or almost every day."*

**Why it works:** Daily challenge makes difficult conversations normal rather than exceptional. It forces issues to be addressed when they're small, prevents the accumulation of unaddressed problems, and keeps the leader honest about whether standards are actually being held.

**What to teach:** *Address issues same day even if setting a formal conversation for later. Have growth conversations daily. Raise concerns immediately. Give specific, timely feedback. Make tough talks routine.*

**Why it's natural for you:** D=92, Competitive=85, Interaction=80, Commanding P1. Your wiring doesn't avoid difficult conversations; it finds them clarifying. The combination of directness and people-orientation means your challenges land as investment rather than hostility. That is the hardest pattern to teach in coaching and the one most of your peers struggle most with.

### 3. Enable your leaders to reach their full potential *(Q137)*

**The standard:** *"My goal is to enable my leaders to reach their full potential."*

**Why it works:** Leaders who orient around their people's ceiling rather than their own performance create sustainable development cultures. The orientation shapes language, delegation, and resource decisions downstream.

**What to teach:** Framing leadership as elevation of others. Making that orientation visible in your communication.

**Why it's natural for you:** Your Altruistic driver (P3, 57) — "driven to assist others for a specific purpose oriented to the broader good" — is the engine here. You develop leaders because developing them serves a larger mission you care about, not because individual development is its own reward. That broader-good framing is contagious and teachable.

### 4. Focus on impact over perception *(Q131)*

**The standard:** Your leadership is admired because you focus on impact rather than perception — you make necessary decisions without hesitation and act from conviction rather than consensus.

**Why it works:** Leaders who lead by impact set a tone where the team orients around outcomes rather than optics. It's rare, immediately legible, and profoundly stabilizing for the organization.

**What to teach:** *"Makes necessary decisions without hesitation. Takes unpopular stands on principle. Acts from conviction rather than consensus. Puts improvement before approval."* — lift verbatim.

**Why it's natural for you:** D=92 wires you for conviction-based action. Commanding (P1, 65) gives you the independence-orientation that doesn't need consensus to move. Low-C (14) means you're not waiting for perfect information before acting. This is probably the cleanest wiring-to-standard match in your whole profile.

### 5. Your direct reports are fully capable *(Q138)*

**The standard:** *"Each of my direct reports is fully capable of doing their job without supervision or guidance."*

**Why it works:** The belief the leader holds about the team's capability is the ceiling on what the team ever achieves. Leaders who believe their reports are capable make it true; leaders who don't undermine it.

**What to teach:** How to see capability in advance of it being fully proven. The practices that communicate the belief in day-to-day interactions.

**Why it's natural for you:** This is held up by Replacing Self (+2.80 — your largest category strength). Your low-C (14) and Frequent Change (85) mean you genuinely do not want to be in the operation, and your Altruistic and Selfless drivers want the team to carry the mission. That combination produces the standard naturally. Isaiah, by comparison, scores at the opposite extreme on this — his C=81 and Persistence=90 keep him in every decision. You are the exemplar.

### 6. Grow by taking informed risks *(Q68)*

**The standard:** You prefer a low-probability path with high growth potential over a high-probability path with limited upside.

**Why it works:** Organizations grow on the back of leaders who accept asymmetric bets when the upside is meaningful. Risk-aversion at the top propagates downward and caps growth.

**What to teach:** The intuition for asymmetric payoffs. How to distinguish informed risk from reckless risk.

**Why it's natural for you:** D=92 and Urgency=92 are wired for bold moves. Commanding (P1) is independence-oriented — you don't need cover before moving. Your track record is the case study.

### 7. Zero voluntary attrition *(Q50)*

**The standard:** *"There has been virtually zero voluntary attrition on my team in the last year. My team members turn down higher offers from other companies."*

**Why it works:** When top talent consistently turns down higher offers, you've built an environment where growth and meaning outpace compensation. The standard comes not from comfort but from the opportunity structure the leader creates.

**What to teach:** *"Create compelling growth paths. Offer opportunities they can't get elsewhere. Make the team the best place to learn. Challenge top performers constantly. Invest in development proactively. Build deeper than transactional loyalty. Make leaving a clear step down."*

**Why it's natural for you:** Interaction=80, People-Oriented=70, and Altruistic (P3) combine into genuine retention-through-meaning. Your DI profile makes you someone people *want* to work for. The data confirms this is a teachable strength — you can articulate what creates the "better place to be" even when peers can't.

### 8. Encourage consistent disagreement *(Q102)*

**The standard:** *"I encourage and foster consistent disagreement in my meetings."*

**Why it works:** Leaders who require disagreement clarify their own thinking and surface the counterarguments needed for quality decisions. Disagreement is the cornerstone of decision quality; without it leaders operate in a vacuum of false consensus.

**What to teach:** Specific practices that reward disagreement — asking people to expound their reasoning before debating the merits, thanking dissenters in public, visibly changing your mind when their argument is better.

**Why it's natural for you:** I=72 and D=92 mean you can hold your ground and welcome challenge simultaneously without either collapsing. Receptive (P2, 60) literally values new ideas and methods — disagreement IS methodology exploration for you. This is a pattern that high-C introverted leaders frequently cannot produce.

### 9. Remove misaligned team members quickly *(Q120)*

**The standard:** *"When a team member can't surrender their distaste for the leader and organization, they must be removed quickly."*

**Why it works:** Misaligned team members pull others. Quick action protects the rest of the team; delay signals tolerance and erodes the standard.

**What to teach:** How to distinguish disagreement (healthy) from disloyalty (terminal). The courage to move fast without becoming paranoid.

**Why it's natural for you:** D=92 and Commanding (P1) produce decisive people-action. The combination of willingness to act and the Altruistic frame (serving the broader good means not letting one person undermine the mission) carries the standard without sentimentality.

### 10. Success is about growth, contribution, and impact *(Q115)*

**The standard:** A person is successful if they have grown, contributed, and measurably affected others — not by extrinsic markers.

**Why it works:** The definition of success that the leader holds propagates. Leaders who define success by extrinsic markers get teams that optimize for optics; leaders who define it by contribution get teams that optimize for impact.

**What to teach:** Your operational definition of success, the way you evaluate people against it, and the language you use to name it.

**Why it's natural for you:** Altruistic (P3) is oriented to broader-good impact as the measure of worth. Commanding (P1) in its mature expression values recognition through genuine achievement, not appearance. The definition of success your profile produces is the right one.

## Part 2 — What to work on

Ten standards where the current expression is furthest from the research-established behavior. The routines here are not about becoming a different kind of leader. They are about installing specific practices where your wiring does not naturally deliver — using your actual Primary drivers as fuel. Because Intentional is Indifferent for you, most of these routines will be framed around Altruistic (broader-good) rather than individual relationships, and Receptive (methodology exploration) rather than structure.

### 1. Don't make irreversible decisions until you have to *(Q123 — Impact #1)*

**The standard:** *"I don't make an irreversible decision until I have to. I prize the optionality inherent in studying a problem and not acting until it is necessary."*

**Why it matters:** Every major decision has a hidden clock. Good decision timing takes full advantage of changes in the marketplace, organizational assets, and key relationships. The best leaders defer deliberately — acquiring more data, engaging stakeholders more fully, and gaining more understanding — and then act vigorously when the window calls for it. The costliest decisions are made before they need to be.

**What to do:** Before any irreversible decision, ask explicitly: *"What is the actual deadline? What information arrives between now and then? Who hasn't weighed in yet?"* Build a default of deferral for any decision that is genuinely irreversible.

**Your profile:** Urgency=92 and D=92 mean your baseline move is decide-now. C=14 means you don't want to swim in detail waiting for more. Every cell of your wiring pushes against optionality-preservation. This is the starkest cell of the "not natural" quadrant in your whole profile — answered 5 where ideal is 1 (full disagreement with the standard you'd ideally hold). **Fuel: Receptive (60, P2).** Frame the deferral as methodology exploration — *what would I learn if I waited?* That is a Receptive question, not a patience question, and your P2 will sustain it.

### 2. Third-party compliments create followership *(Q132 — Impact #2)*

**The standard:** When you see someone do exceptional work, what creates more followership is telling *others* about them — not direct praise.

**Why it matters:** Direct praise from a leader carries an implicit "but" and can feel transactional. Telling someone else about a person's excellence — someone who will mention it back to them — lands as genuine fanship because it cost the leader something (time, relationship capital) to spread the word. It becomes reputation rather than feedback.

**What to do:** *"Share praise through others connected to the person. Tell stories of excellence to colleagues. Create reputation-building moments. Spread positive feedback through networks. Build others up when they're not present."*

**Your profile:** Your Demonstrating Genuine Fanness score is **−2.59, the single largest sub-dimension gap in your profile.** Intentional is Indifferent (28), which is exactly the driver that would otherwise carry specific appreciation of specific people. Your profile admires crowds and causes more naturally than it celebrates specific individuals. **Fuel: Altruistic (57, P3).** Frame third-party praise as a *broader-good investment* — the reputation of excellent people in your network is a public asset you can create, and your status (Commanding P1) makes your opinion carry disproportionate weight with others. Each compliment you place is a durable signal that outlives the conversation.

### 3. Delineate small changes, not big ones *(Q95 — Impact #3)*

**The standard:** *"To help people improve, I prefer delineating small changes."*

**Why it matters:** Ambitious goals motivate teams; ambitious individual habit-change goals fail. When people underestimate the difficulty of habit change and set unrealistic targets, they fail after a few setbacks and revert to the old norm — reinforcing the behavior they wanted to break. Small, attainable changes stack into large ones. This is the single most reliable finding in behavioral change research.

**What to do:** *"Delineate small changes to help people improve, not big ones."* When coaching someone, resist the urge to name the large transformation; name the next 5% move.

**Your profile:** D=92 and Urgency=92 both pull you toward ambitious framing. Your own track record of large moves reinforces the pattern. But what worked at the organization level (bold bets) is the wrong template for individual coaching conversations. **Fuel: Receptive (60, P2) + Selfless (54, P4).** Frame this as methodology respect — you are *letting the person's pace drive the change* rather than imposing your pace on their habit formation. Selfless does the slower, more patient work; Receptive honors that each person's methodology is different from yours.

### 4. Action over inaction *(Q385 — Impact #4)*

**The standard:** *"The best you can do is the right thing, the next best thing is the wrong thing, and the worst thing you can do is nothing."* Bias to action.

**Why it matters:** The cost of inaction almost always exceeds the cost of wrong action. Wrong decisions create movement, learning, and clarity; inaction compounds problems while masquerading as prudence. Problems don't age well; opportunities don't pause.

**What to do:** *"Make decisions when needed. Choose action over inaction. Create forward momentum daily. Learn through doing. Correct course while moving."*

**Your profile:** The paradox: your Urgency=92 and D=92 should make this natural, and historically it was. You answered 5 (disagreement) where the ideal is 1 (strong agreement). At 83 and in academia, you may have internalized a more contemplative stance that is appropriate to your current setting but reads as ineffectiveness against a CEO-calibrated standard. This Impact gap is less about installing a behavior you don't have and more about *remembering the wiring you still have* — it hasn't gone anywhere. **Fuel: Commanding (65, P1) + Altruistic.** This is the one impact item where Commanding earns its keep as fuel rather than being the source of the gap. Your broader-good orientation is served by movement, not contemplation.

### 5. Don't be the one creating the urgency *(Q81 — Impact #5)*

**The standard:** *"I am not the one creating the urgency in any departments. Areas of the business aren't dependent on me for pace."*

**Why it matters:** Top-down urgency creates dependency and reactivity. Intrinsic urgency distributed throughout the organization creates responsiveness and ownership. Urgency cannot really be taught — it must be *selected for* in hiring and reinforced through standards rather than driven from above.

**What to do:** *"Enshrine that teams own their pace and energy. Hire for intrinsic urgency."* Practice withholding your urgency so theirs can show up.

**Your profile:** Urgency=92 is the single highest score in your Behavioral Hierarchy. Commanding (P1) is structurally about driving from a position of authority. These two together make it genuinely hard for you not to be the urgency source. You answered 4 where ideal is 1 — a significant gap. **Fuel: Receptive (60, P2).** Frame withholding your urgency as *letting their methodology emerge*. The urgency they produce when you withhold yours is data about how they think — and your Receptive driver values exactly that kind of data.

### 6. Single-point accountability, not shared *(Q384 — Impact #6)*

**The standard:** When multiple people are accountable to the same problem or outcome, results are typically *worse*, not better. Accountability should land on a single owner who collaborates with others but feels the full weight of responsibility.

**Why it matters:** Shared accountability causes diffusion of responsibility and effort. True individual accountability, paired with collaborative input, produces better outcomes and more reliable execution than committee-style ownership.

**What to do:** *"Assign clear decision rights. Make one person accountable. Define specific responsibilities. Separate actions from outcomes. Create clear ownership."*

**Your profile:** Clarity of Accountability is at −1.19 and the flag "Clarity of Accountability | Hi" is lit, meaning it's flagged at the severe end. Your collaborative instincts (you answered 2 — toward multiple people good) tend toward shared ownership rather than single-point accountability. **Fuel: Altruistic (57, P3).** Frame single-point accountability as *protecting the person who needs to succeed.* When you assign accountability to one person clearly, you are giving them the information they need to do their job — anything less is withholding clarity. That's a broader-good frame your P3 sustains.

### 7. Resist involvement until truly needed *(Q31 — Impact #7)*

**The standard:** *"I resist being involved in the actual operation or process of getting things done until I have to — and when I do, I go all-in."*

**Why it matters:** Hands-off and eyes-on until truly needed; then full engagement. The pattern of restraint-then-vigor is different from either chronic intervention or chronic absence. Both extremes fail.

**What to do:** *"Trust teams to execute. Let others own their work. Stay out until absolutely needed. Go all-in when truly required."*

**Your profile:** Your Replacing Self is +2.80 — you generally *can* step back. But the flag "EyesOff, HandsOn | Severe" is lit, and your Competitive=85, Urgency=92 means the impulse to re-engage is strong when you see something worth engaging with. Answered 4 where ideal is 1. **Fuel: Selfless (54, P4).** Frame restraint as *letting them have the win.* Every time you intervene you remove a learning moment from someone else; every time you withhold you create one. Your Selfless driver values that kind of team-serving restraint.

### 8. Fight for the right people *(Q34 — Impact #8)*

**The standard:** *"There are no people on my team who, if I'm told they're leaving, I wouldn't fight hard to keep."*

**Why it matters:** Your response to someone leaving tells you everything about whether they should have already left. If relief rather than concern is the first reaction, you've been tolerating performance that doesn't meet your standard. Your strongest performers watch how long you keep people you wouldn't fight for, and they calibrate accordingly.

**What to do:** *"Address performance issues immediately when spotted. Have clear conversations about gaps with team members. Move quickly on needed changes. Make tough personnel decisions rather than accepting adequacy."*

**Your profile:** Answered 2 where ideal is 5 (reversed polarity — ideal is disagreement with the statement). You would not fight to keep at least some current team members. The flag "Satisfied with Average" is lit. Your People-Oriented=70 and high Interaction may make you more tolerant of average performers than the standard calls for. **Fuel: Altruistic (57, P3).** Frame the standard as *protecting the high performers* — every tolerated gap is a signal to them about what's acceptable. The broader-good framing carries this better than "have harder conversations" would.

### 9. Growth at every level, not bottom-10% replacement *(Q33 — Impact #9)*

**The standard:** The question of trading out the bottom 10% is the wrong frame. The right frame is commitment to growth at every level — *"especially the best get better"* — and action on misalignment when it appears.

**Why it matters:** Replace-the-bottom thinking is a basketball frame (swap players for better ones). Building great organizations is a crew frame (everyone rows together with a shared commitment to improvement). The test is whether people share the team's commitment to growth, not whether better replacements exist.

**What to do:** Reframe personnel decisions around commitment-to-growth rather than current-performance. Hold the standard that *"A players and below have to get better to have a seat on this team"* — the best get better.

**Your profile:** Answered 2 where ideal is 5 (disagreement with the replace-frame). Your high-D / Commanding profile can default to the replace-frame when the growth-frame would serve better. **Fuel: Altruistic (57).** The broader-good frame — *commitment to growth serves the organization in a way that replacement can never match* — is exactly your P3's register.

### 10. Respect your followers before requiring their respect *(Q602 — Impact #10)*

**The standard:** What matters most in leader-follower respect is the leader respecting the followers, not the reverse. True influence comes from genuinely respecting and valuing people first.

**Why it matters:** Leaders focused on *being respected* erode engagement; leaders who *show respect* create cultures where respect for them naturally follows. The admired leader version of this goes one step further — admiring people in a way disconnected from their performance, valuing them as whole people not just contributors.

**What to do:** *"Creates value, not approval. Makes unpopular decisions when needed. Chooses impact over image. Puts team success before personal status. Acts from conviction rather than consensus."* Treat followers as the more important party in the relationship.

**Your profile:** Answered 2 where ideal is 5. Your stature and Commanding (P1, 65) naturally pull you toward being-respected-as-leader. The routine asks you to invert the orientation — to hold the followers as primary. **Fuel: Altruistic (57, P3) + Selfless (54, P4).** Frame this as *your legacy is what they become*, not what they remember about you. That frame makes your P3 and P4 the engines of a behavior your P1 would otherwise pull against.

## How the two lists connect

Seven of your top ten teach items are about *pushing or admiring* — daily challenge, impact-over-perception, encouraging disagreement, growth bets, capable reports, people who won't leave, removing the misaligned. One is about the frame of leadership itself (definition of success). Two are about presence (time with customers, enabling leaders). These are the behaviors your DI-profile and your Commanding-Altruistic-Selfless driver cluster produce effortlessly at teaching-level.

Nine of your top ten impact items are about *holding or clarifying* — deferring decisions, specific appreciation, small changes, withholding urgency, clear accountability, restraint, genuine fights, growth over replacement, respect-for. These are holding moves your wiring does not natively produce. In your current role — academia at 83 with gripes — the holding moves are exactly what would raise effectiveness the most.

The coaching path is therefore not to become a different kind of leader. It is to use the pushing strengths you already teach as the foundation on which the holding routines get built — carried not by the patience you are not wired for, but by the Receptive curiosity, Altruistic broader-good, and Selfless team-utility that actually energize you.

Your Intentional driver being Indifferent is worth revisiting explicitly. Many coaching models lean on specific-person investment as the lever for developmental behaviors. That lever won't work for you and we should stop looking for it. Your development lever is the mission or cause the specific person serves, not the specific person themselves. That frame reshapes Routines 2, 3, 6, 7, 8, 9, and 10 — all of them reach you more through "this serves something larger" than through "this person needs you."

## A closing note

Your wiring made you the leader you became, and it continues to produce teaching-level mastery in the behaviors that wiring naturally creates. The impact list above names ten specific places where the next practice rep lives — and because the standards are universal, each of those reps can be taken anywhere in your life where you have influence.

A few of the impact routines in particular travel well beyond any current role. *Believe in people in advance of themselves* — that's as much a standard for being a grandfather as it is for being a CEO, and it's hard to do either well without the practice. *Delineate small changes, not big ones* — that's true whether you're coaching a junior executive or encouraging a great-niece through a health scare. *Respect your followers more than you require their respect* — that's the standard for mentees, for colleagues in the academic community you're part of now, and for anyone you have a legacy conversation with, including yourself.

The instrument is responsive to deliberate practice. Leaders who take the impact routines seriously see their scores move meaningfully within a coaching engagement rather than across a career. The routines above are fueled by your actual drivers — Receptive, Altruistic, Selfless — rather than by willpower, which is why they're likely to hold.

Especially the best get better. You already know this — it's why you've spent the last decade writing and teaching about it. The guide is here as an invitation to keep doing what you've always done, with one more tool to do it precisely.
