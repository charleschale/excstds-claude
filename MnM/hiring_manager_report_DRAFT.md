## Recommendation

**Recommend against hire.** Three of the four signals in our combined ExcStds + TTI data that have historically required significantly longer than a normal coaching timeframe to shift are present in this file, each independently corroborated by multiple data sources. These signals are not uncoachable — every behavior in the Excellence Standards framework can be developed with deliberate practice — but in our experience they require years rather than months to move, which exceeds what a typical onboarding and early-tenure coaching engagement can accommodate.

Great teams are rowing, not basketball. The crew is only as fast as its slowest rower, and every hire raises or lowers the boat's pace. Read against the three-axes framework we use for evaluation:

- **Talent (capacity to grow) — low.** The slow-to-move signals are specifically talent-axis constraints within the hiring window. Her TTI Intellectual DF is extremely high (94), so she will grow deeply in knowledge domains — but the leadership-behavior growth trajectory looks flat over the relevant timeframe.
- **Judgment (everyday decisions at the table) — poor.** ExcStds Organizational Decision Making at −2.83, Clarity of Accountability −2.28, Facts Over Feelings −2.24. TTI Instinctive DF at 0 corroborates: she will not use pattern-matching to move decisions forward.
- **Skills (ability to do the job) — not measured by these instruments; likely strong for Financial Reporting based on TTI wiring (C=71/78, Analysis 75, Persistence 80, Consistent 78).** Validate separately in technical interview.

Most hiring managers over-emphasize skills and under-estimate talent and judgment. Her profile is the classic version of that mistake: strong technical skills, weak talent for leadership growth in the window, and weak daily judgment. Hiring against this combination historically drags team pace.

If the hire is being revisited, the structured probes in the final section target the specific answers most affecting this conclusion. We would not expect the interview to reverse the recommendation, but the path exists.

## Evaluation on three axes

She will read deeply, study systems thoroughly, and become more competent technically over time. That is real talent and should not be dismissed.

Her capacity to grow in *leadership behaviors* within a normal coaching timeframe, however, is constrained. Three of the four slow-to-move signals we track are present (detailed in the next section), each corroborated by TTI wiring evidence rather than self-report alone. Wiring-level constraints — low D, low Urgency BH, Instinctive DF at 0 — describe stable patterns that do not typically move meaningfully in the first one to two years of a role.

### Judgment — everyday decisions at the table

**Poor.** ExcStds Organizational Decision Making at −2.83 is her second-worst L1 category. Sub-dimensions: Clarity of Accountability −2.28, Facts Over Feelings −2.24, Respects Collective Wisdom −1.09. She answered Q382 and Q384 — both directly about accountability structure — toward diffuse/shared accountability rather than single-point ownership. The *Clarity of Accountability | Hi* flag is lit severely.

Her Instinctive DF is 0 — she will never use pattern-matching or gut-based calls — and the TTI narrative is explicit: *"She feels tension when forced to make major decisions quickly,"* *"usually becomes cautious about the bigger decisions,"* *"tends to postpone the decision-making process until she has all the facts."* Financial Reporting work requires the inverse: facts over preferences, at speed, with clear ownership. The judgment axis is the most load-bearing concern for this specific role.

### Skills — ability to do the job

**Not measured by these instruments; validate separately.** The TTI wiring suggests the raw ingredients for strong Financial Reporting technical skills are present: C=71 Natural / 78 Adapted (quality orientation, rule-following, attention to detail), Analysis BH=75, Organized Workplace=80, Persistence=80, Consistent=78, Following Policy=75. The three-axes rule: skills must be present several levels down to be effective. A Financial Reporting Manager must have the reporting skills of a Senior IC plus the skills of the ICs supervised. Given her profile, the IC-level skills are likely her strongest dimension. The open question is whether the supervisory skills — delegation, coaching, quality-control-of-others — are present, given the leadership wiring gaps named above. The structured probes target this.

Reminder: strong skills do not compensate for weak talent or weak judgment. The hiring manager should not use her likely technical strength to override the talent and judgment concerns.

## The four slow-to-move signals — decisive evidence

We track four signals in the combined ExcStds + TTI data that, in our experience, require significantly longer than a normal coaching engagement to shift. These signals are not defects and are not uncoachable — the Excellence Standards framework holds that every behavior can be developed with deliberate practice. What these signals require is a longer timeline than the business can typically wait for. Three of the four are present in Meriste's file, and each is corroborated across ExcStds and TTI rather than resting on a single data point.

**1. Deliberate Urgency — ExcStds Z|Algo −2.64; TTI Natural D=38; TTI BH Urgency rank #12 of 12 (score 34).** Three independent data sources agree: low urgency is wiring, not context. Sub-dimensions within Deliberate Urgency: Action Over Inaction −1.96, Extreme Proactivity −0.88, Proactive Belief in People −0.57, Fire in Belly −0.27. On Q385 ("the worst thing you can do is nothing") she answered toward inaction. On Q49 ("I am so proactive that I almost never get tasked") she rejected the statement. The TTI "Areas for Improvement" section corroborates in the report's own words: *"Hesitate to act without precedent"* and *"Lean on other managers if information and direction is not clear."* For a Financial Reporting Manager role that requires proactive close management, ahead-of-the-audit anticipation, and rapid response when reporting issues surface, this is the most load-bearing concern in the file. Movement on urgency comes with sustained deliberate practice, but in our experience the timeline is years rather than months — and the Quantum role needs the behavior present now.

**2. Organizational Decision Making — ExcStds Z|Algo −2.83; TTI Instinctive DF=0; TTI narrative corroborates.** Sub-dimensions noted above. The TTI Instinctive DF at 0 is the decisive corroborator: she will not use pattern-matching or gut-based decisions. Decision speed is wiring-constrained, not skill-limited. Combined with Clarity of Accountability flag lit severely, the judgment axis signal is strong. Same timeline note: this can move with deliberate practice, but the movement takes longer than the role allows.

**3. Conditional Belief in Others — Severe flag lit.** Five separate data points describe the same pattern: the *Conditional Belief Severe* flag itself, the *Upgrade Team* flag, the *Team Unqualified* flag, Q124 rejected (she does not see her direct reports as a dream team), and TTI Altruistic DF at 7 (Indifferent) — she invests in others for specific purposes, not unconditionally. This is a candidate who sees her people as conditional assets rather than as people to believe in advance of their performance. Belief-in-advance can be developed, but the frame shift is slow work measured in years of deliberate practice across multiple situations.

**4. Satisfied with Gripes — flag lit.** Corroborated by *Unsatisfied But Tolerate Cs/Ds* flag. TTI corroborates with *"Whenever possible, Meriste avoids face-to-face conflict"* and *"Yield her position to avoid controversy"* — high-S wiring combined with conflict-avoidance produces the "tolerates gripes rather than addresses them" pattern. Confronting rather than tolerating chronic complaining is teachable, but requires sustained practice against the wiring.

The combined presence of these four signals is not four independent weak scores. It is an internally consistent pattern describing a candidate who does not operate ahead of direction, does not structure clear ownership, holds her team in conditional regard, and tolerates chronic complaining — with TTI wiring corroborating each. The coaching question is not *can* she improve (she can) but *will she improve in time for the role to succeed* (our experience says no).

## Daily-work fit versus the standards

The TTI wiring data and the ExcStds standards data answer two different questions. Keeping them separated matters, because conflating them produces a role-based mediocrity pass that the Excellence Standards are designed to resist.

**What the TTI tells us — the daily activities she will do well and those she will find depleting:**

*Aligned activities (she will execute these well):* Precise analytical work, data reconciliation, rule-following and quality assurance, deep research and learning, persistent task completion, supporting-role execution, working for a well-defined leader and cause, long-horizon single objectives. Supported by: C=71/78, Analysis BH=75, Organized Workplace=80, Persistence=80, Consistent=78, Intellectual DF=94, Collaborative DF=75.

*Depleting activities (she will struggle to execute or will avoid):* Rapid decisions under partial information, direct confrontation, driving team pace from the front, holding status or authority, proactive initiation without precedent, short-term multifaceted projects. Supported by: low D=38, Urgency BH=34, Interaction BH=40, Competitive BH=40, Commanding DF=11, Instinctive DF=0. TTI "Areas for Improvement" names these directly: *"Prefer things to people,"* *"Lean on other managers if information and direction is not clear,"* *"Hesitate to act without precedent,"* *"Yield her position to avoid controversy."*

**What the Excellence Standards tell us — the quality of her leadership execution in any role:**

The four slow-to-move signals describe how she leads whatever sphere of influence she is placed in. They apply in an IC role, a manager role, a cross-functional project lead role, and in her life outside work. They do not relax based on scope.

**How the two read together for the Quantum role:**

The alignment between her wiring and the daily activities of a senior Financial Reporting position is real and genuine. She will likely produce accurate, thorough, well-documented reporting work — that is a strength.

The four slow-to-move signals describe how she will *execute* that work and *influence others* around it. She will do the work carefully and slowly; she will prefer to study a question rather than call it; she will struggle to hold the team around her to a higher standard; she will tolerate rather than address chronic complaining. The standard does not relax for IC roles — a low-urgency IC is still a mediocre IC — and the hiring manager should decide consciously about whether that execution pattern raises or lowers the rowing pace of the team, rather than through the assumption that "IC roles don't need these behaviors."

## Structured interview follow-up

If the hire remains under consideration, these probes target the specific answers most affecting the recommendation. The probes are designed to distinguish genuine candidate strength from self-report artifact. We would not expect the interview to reverse the recommendation, but the path exists.

1. **On urgency and initiative (Q49, Q385; corroborated by TTI D=38, Urgency BH=34).** "Walk me through the last three months. Give me a concrete example of something you acted on before being asked — what signal did you see that others didn't, what did you do, what was the result?" Listen for specificity. A generic "I always try to be proactive" answer confirms the instrument. A concrete story with named colleagues, dates, and outcomes would be counter-evidence.

2. **On accountability structure (Q382, Q384; corroborated by TTI Instinctive=0).** "When you have a reporting deadline and three people could be involved, how do you structure ownership? Describe the last time that worked well, and the last time it didn't." Listen for single-point-accountability language. The instrument says she prefers shared ownership; a crisp "one owner, clear escalation path" answer would be counter-evidence.

3. **On her current team (Q124, Q50, Q120; corroborated by *Upgrade Team* and *Team Unqualified* flags).** "Describe your current team. If one of them told you tomorrow they were leaving for a 20% raise, who would you fight to keep and what specifically would you say to them?" The instrument says the answer is "no one." Listen for whether she names specific people with specific strengths.

4. **On effort vs. talent (Q65).** She answered at the extreme "I am smart" end of a 1–5 scale where the ideal is "I work hard." The TTI "Strengths & Weaknesses" section corroborates: *"She tends to allow return on investment to supersede purpose and logic."* Probe directly: "Tell me about something you had to work hard at that didn't come naturally. How long did you stay with it and what changed?" Listen for an effort-orientation grounded in a real story, not a stock answer.

5. **On difficult conversations (Not Pleasing category; corroborated by TTI "Yield her position to avoid controversy").** "Tell me about a time you gave feedback someone didn't want to hear. What did you say, what did they say, what happened next?" Listen for whether she engaged or deferred. A deferred-to-HR or deferred-to-next-review answer confirms the instrument.

6. **On the Quantum role specifically, with the role-fit question surfaced.** "Walk me through your first 90 days. What would you prioritize, what would you leave alone, and how would you know you were on track? Then: in your ideal version of this role, how much of your time is doing reporting work yourself versus developing and managing your team?" The second half is the decisive question. If she answers heavily toward doing the work herself, the TTI profile is operating as predicted — she is motivationally oriented to the IC portion of the work. If she answers heavily toward developing the team, either she is performing in the interview or the instrument is wrong. Listen carefully.

7. **On hiring itself.** "If you got this role, who is the first person you would hire onto your team and what bar would you hold them to?" Listen for *Upgrade Team* and *Conditional Belief* signals either confirmed or contradicted. Vague "a good culture fit" answers confirm the instrument; specific high-bar language would push back on it.

## Data caveats

**What this report can tell you:**

- How this candidate's leadership self-report and wiring align with patterns observed across thousands of leaders in the Hale dataset.
- Which specific behaviors appear strongest and weakest, with TTI corroboration for each.
- Where coaching investment has historically produced movement quickly and where it has not.
- Whether her wiring fits the daily activities of a management role versus a senior-IC role.

**What this report cannot tell you:**

- Her technical competence in financial reporting. The instruments do not measure domain skill. Given her TTI profile, technical competence is the most likely source of genuine strength and should be validated independently. Remember that strong skills alone do not compensate for weak talent and weak judgment in our experience.
- Whether her ExcStds scoring reflects actual behavior or self-perception. Low self-insight can produce either inflated or deflated scores. The structured probes above are designed to help distinguish.
- Whether environmental factors in her current role are suppressing her ExcStds scores. The TTI, which measures stable wiring rather than situational self-report, argues that the ExcStds pattern is mostly wiring-driven and is unlikely to shift materially on role change.
